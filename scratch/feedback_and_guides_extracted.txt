[Heading3]: 4.2 Risk Colour Bands
[Normal]: Segments are colour-coded by their highest relevant risk band so you can quickly spot high-risk areas.
[Normal]: PSAT calculates four independent risk scores per segment:
[Normal]: | Score | Crash Type |
[Normal]: |---|---|
[Normal]: | BB | Bicyclist–Bicyclist |
[Normal]: | BP | Bicyclist–Pedestrian |
[Normal]: | SB | Single Bicyclist (departure/fall) |
[Normal]: | VB | Vehicle–Bicyclist |
[Heading4]: Risk Bands for BB, BP, SB
[Normal]: | Band | Label | Score Range | Map Colour | Hex |
[Normal]: |---|---|---|---|---|
[Normal]: | 1 | Low | < 5 | 🟢 Green | `#87C424` |
[Normal]: | 2 | Medium | 5 – 10 | 🟡 Yellow | `#FFCC1A` |
[Normal]: | 3 | High | 10 – 20 | 🟠 Orange | `#FF5B1A` |
[Normal]: | 4 | Extreme | > 20 | 🟣 Purple | `#CD1AFF` |
[Heading4]: Risk Bands for VB (Vehicle–Bicyclist)
[Normal]: | Band | Label | Score Range | Map Colour | Hex |
[Normal]: |---|---|---|---|---|
[Normal]: | 1 | Low | < 10 | 🟢 Green | `#87C424` |
[Normal]: | 2 | Medium | 10 – 25 | 🟡 Yellow | `#FFCC1A` |
[Normal]: | 3 | High | 25 – 60 | 🟠 Orange | `#FF5B1A` |
[Normal]: | 4 | Extreme | > 60 | 🟣 Purple | `#CD1AFF` |
[Normal]: > Overall Risk Level Band = the highest band across BB, BP, SB, and VB for that segment. A single Extreme sub-score makes the overall band Extreme regardless of the others.

[Heading3]: 3.4 Auto-Code Options
[Normal]: PSAT supports three auto-code methods that can be run individually or in combination:
[ListBullet]: **CV auto-code** — reads the segment image using computer vision models
[ListBullet]: **GIS auto-code** — reads GIS shapefiles for the segment location
[ListBullet]: **Logic rules** — applies cascade rules based on detected surface types
[ListBullet]: **Bulk auto-code** — runs across all selected rows or the full project
[ListBullet]: **Per-attribute auto-code** — targets only certain attributes
[Normal]: Autocode progress is tracked in the project listing as Percentage Segments Autocoded.
[Heading4]: 3.4.1 Attributes Coded by CV (Image Analysis)
[Normal]: The following attributes are automatically inferred from street-level photographs using YOLO computer vision models:
[Normal]: | Attribute | How CV Determines the Value |
[Normal]: |---|---|
[Normal]: | Facility Type | Off-road bicycle classifier + fixed obstacle decision logic |
[Normal]: | Light Segregation | Set to Present by default when any path is detected |
[Normal]: | Adjacent Road Lane 0–1m | Adjacent road classifier (confidence ≥ 0.8) |
[Normal]: | Adjacent Road Lane 1–3m | Adjacent road classifier (confidence ≥ 0.8) |
[Normal]: | Adjacent Object/Level Change 0–1m | Inferred from Adjacent Road Lane 0–1m result |
[Normal]: | Adjacent Object/Level Change 1–3m | Inferred from Adjacent Road Lane 1–3m result |
[Normal]: | Adjacent Sidewalk 0–1m | Multi-path detection (multiple path segments visible) |
[Normal]: | Fixed Obstacle on Facility | Fixed obstacle segmentation model |
[Normal]: | Non-Fixed Obstacle on Facility | Fixed obstacle segmentation (label 9 = non-fixed) |
[Normal]: | Delineation | Delineation classifier model |
[Heading4]: 3.4.2 Attributes Coded by GIS Layer Mapping
[Normal]: The following attributes are automatically derived from the GIS shapefiles stored in the system:
[Normal]: | Attribute | GIS Layer Used | Buffer / Method |
[Normal]: |---|---|---|
[Normal]: | Area Type (Urban/CBD) | `area_type` polygon | Point-in-polygon containment |
[Normal]: | Area Type (Industrial) | `area_type` polygon | Point-in-polygon containment |
[Normal]: | Area Type (Rural) | `LanduseRural2026` / `rural` polygon | Point-in-polygon containment |
[Normal]: | Area Type (Recreational) | `LanduseRecre2026` / `recreation` polygon | Point-in-polygon containment |
[Normal]: | Area Type (Suburban) | *(No layer match)* | Default fallback |
[Normal]: | Facility Width per Direction | `path` / `CyclingPath_Jul2024` / `FootPath_Mar2025` | Nearest within 5 m |
[Normal]: | Road Operating Speed (mean) | `LinkID_Shape_File` (with speed CSV lookup) | Nearest road link |
[Normal]: | Road Speed Limit | `Speed_limit` | Nearest road link |
[Normal]: | Number of Lanes – Adjacent Road | `kerb_line` | Nearest within 10 m |
[Normal]: | Adjacent Vehicle Parking | `parking_lot` | Within 20 m buffer |
[Normal]: | Peak Pedestrian Flow | `AMGbeforeCount` / `AMGsensorCount` | Within 20 m buffer |
[Normal]: | Peak Bicycle/LV Traffic Flow | `AMGbeforeCount` / `AMGsensorCount` | Within 20 m buffer |
[Normal]: | Intersection or Road Crossing | `roadcrossinglayer` / `AMG_BC2025_shp` | Within 5 m / 2 m buffer |
[Normal]: | Crossing Facility | `AMG_BC2025_shp` | Within 2 m buffer |
[Normal]: | Pedestrian Crossing proximity | `Mrt_exit` / `bus_stop` | Within 20 m buffer |
[Normal]: | Heavy Vehicle Flow | `bus_lane` proximity | Within 20 m buffer |
[Normal]: > Note: Road AADT has no GIS auto-coding path — it must be coded manually.
[Heading4]: 3.4.3 Attributes Coded by Logic Rules
[Normal]: Logic rules apply a cascade system based on what surface types the CV model detects in the image. Each step overrides the previous if its trigger condition is met:
[Normal]: | Step | Trigger Condition | Key Attributes Set |
[Normal]: |---|---|---|
[Normal]: | Step 1 — Default | Always applied first | Facility Type = Sidewalk, Light Seg. = Present, Delineation = Not Present |
[Normal]: | Step 2 — Cycling Path | Cycling/Wet Cycling surface in bottom 20% of image | Facility Type = Off-Road Bicycle Path, Delineation = Present |
[Normal]: | Step 3 — Red Stripe | Red Stripe surface in bottom 20% of image | Facility Type = Multi-Use Path, Delineation = Present |
[Normal]: | Step 4 — Traffic Crossing | Traffic Crossing markings ≥ 80% of bottom 10% | Facility Type = Mixed Traffic, Crossing Facility = Present, Intersection = Present |
[Normal]: | Step 5 — Zebra Crossing | Zebra crossing ≥ 80% of bottom 10% | Same as Step 4, but Lanes on Intersecting Road = 1 |
[Normal]: | Step 6 — Road Surface | Road pixels ≥ 80% of bottom 10% | Facility Type = Mixed Traffic, Light Seg. = Not Present, Adj. Road 0–1m = Present |

[Heading4]: Auto-enable GIS Layers on Analysis Overlay
[Normal]: When you turn on the Analysis Overlay toggle on the coding page map, PSAT automatically enables the Footpath, Cycling Path, and Shared Path GIS layers so the overlay is always shown over visible path geometry. These layers are never auto-disabled — you can manually toggle them off if you do not need them.
[Heading4]: Filtered Segments from Path Analysis
[Normal]: If you navigate to the Coding page directly from Path Analysis (by clicking a segment on the Path Analysis map), the coding page map will show only the segments that were visible in your active filter. The currently selected segment is always shown regardless of the filter. This makes it easier to focus on a specific subset while coding without losing your analysis context.

[Heading3]: 3.8 CycleRAP Reference
[Normal]: The CycleRAP button (next to Coding Guide in the top tab bar) opens the official iRAP CycleRAP page at irap.org/cyclerap in a new browser tab. Use it to look up attribute definitions, scoring rationale, or the full CycleRAP methodology while you are coding.
[Heading4]: What is CycleRAP?
[Normal]: CycleRAP is the international standard for evaluating road and cycling infrastructure safety. It assesses risk for bicyclists and other light mobility users across all facility types — on-road or off-road — without requiring crash data. PSAT's scoring engine is built on the CycleRAP methodology.
[Heading4]: Resources available on the CycleRAP page
[Normal]: Once the page opens, you will find four downloadable or interactive resources:
[Normal]: | Resource | What it contains |
[Normal]: |---|---|
[Normal]: | Download CycleRAP Methodology | The full technical specification — all attributes, scoring multipliers, crash type formulas, and risk band thresholds used in PSAT |
[Normal]: | Download CycleRAP User Guide | A practical step-by-step guide for surveyors and coders on how to apply CycleRAP in the field |
[Normal]: | Explore the CycleRAP Demonstrator Tool | An interactive online tool showing sample assessments and how scores are calculated |
[Normal]: | Where is CycleRAP being used? | A map and list of countries and organisations currently using CycleRAP |
[Heading4]: How to download the CycleRAP Methodology
[Normal]: 1. Click the CycleRAP button on the Coding page — the iRAP CycleRAP page opens in a new tab.
[Normal]: 2. Scroll down until you see the green "Download CycleRAP Methodology here ↗" button.
[Normal]: 3. Click it. The PDF downloads to your default Downloads folder.
[Normal]: 4. Open the PDF to find the full attribute list, scoring multipliers, and risk band definitions that match PSAT's coding attributes.
[Heading4]: How to download the CycleRAP User Guide
[Normal]: 1. On the same CycleRAP page, click the green "Download CycleRAP User Guide here ↗" button (next to the Methodology button).
[Normal]: 2. The User Guide PDF downloads immediately.
[Normal]: 3. This guide explains field-coding procedures and attribute definitions in plain language — useful as a reference while reviewing segments.
[Heading4]: Other resources
[ListBullet]: **Explore the CycleRAP Demonstrator Tool** — click this button to open an interactive tool that walks through a sample assessment and shows how risk scores are calculated from attribute values.
[ListBullet]: **Where is CycleRAP being used?** — shows a global map of deployments; useful context for understanding the methodology's scope and adoption.
[Normal]: > Tip: The attribute names in PSAT map directly to the CycleRAP Methodology. If you are unsure what value to assign to an attribute (e.g. *Facility Width per Direction* or *Peak Pedestrian Flow*), the Methodology PDF contains the exact definitions and example photographs for each option.
[Heading2]: 6. Treatment Application

You can explore which interventions apply to each segment, preview how they would change risk scores, apply them, and compare before-and-after risk distributions — all without permanently altering the underlying survey data until you are ready.

[ListBullet]: Use the **Search** box or **Filter by tag** dropdown to find projects more quickly.
[ListBullet]: You can load multiple projects together to compare or apply treatments across them in one session.

[Heading3]: Step 2: Understanding the workspace layout
[Normal]: After loading, the workspace is divided into several areas:
[ListBullet]: **Project tabs** (shown at the top when you loaded more than one project) — click a tab to jump to the first segment of that project.
[ListBullet]: **Before Treatment** and **After Treatment maps** — a side-by-side pair of maps at the top of the page, showing all loaded segments colour-coded by risk level. The right map updates in real time as you apply treatments.
[ListBullet]: **A three-column panel** below the maps — Treatment Options on the left, the segment street-level image in the middle, and the Scores & Attributes panel on the right.
[ListBullet]: **An Overall Treatment Analysis section** at the bottom with pie charts comparing the project-wide risk band distribution before and after your treatments.
[Normal]: The page number shown at the top right (e.g. `3 / 120`) tells you which segment you are currently viewing out of the total. Use the Previous and Next buttons, or type a page number directly, to jump to any segment.

[Heading3]: Step 3: Choose By Segment or By Treatment view
[Normal]: The Treatment Options panel on the left has a dropdown that lets you switch between two views:
[Normal]: | View | What it shows | Best used when |
[Normal]: |---|---|---|
[Normal]: | By Segment | Only the treatments that are relevant to the segment you are currently viewing, sorted by how much each treatment would reduce the risk score (most improvement first) | You want to work through the path segment-by-segment and decide on the best fix for each one |
[Normal]: | By Treatment | All treatments that are applicable to at least one segment across the entire loaded project, sorted by how many segments each treatment improves | You want to roll out one improvement (e.g. adding signage) across as many segments as possible in one action |
[Normal]: ____________________
[Heading3]: Step 4: Select and preview treatments
[Normal]: Tick the checkbox next to one or more treatments to select them. Selected treatments are highlighted in the panel.
[Normal]: As soon as you tick a treatment, the Scores card on the right will show a live preview of what the risk scores would look like if you applied those treatments to the current segment. The preview updates automatically whenever you change your selection — you do not need to click anything to trigger it.
[Normal]: The attributes panel also has a Show Post-Treatment toggle. Switch this on to see exactly which attribute values would change if the selected treatments were applied, so you can understand the reasoning behind the score change.
[Normal]: > Note: Selecting treatments only previews the effect — nothing is saved until you click Apply.
[Normal]: ____________________
[Heading3]: Step 5: Apply treatments
[Normal]: When you are satisfied with your selection, click Apply (N) at the bottom of the Treatment Options panel (where *N* is the number of treatments selected):
[ListBullet]: **In By Segment mode**, Apply saves the selected treatments for the current segment only. The treatment colours on the After Treatment map will update for that segment immediately.
[ListBullet]: **In By Treatment mode**, Apply triggers a confirmation dialog listing each selected treatment and asking whether to apply it to **all eligible segments** across the loaded project. Confirm to apply in bulk, or cancel to go back.
[Normal]: If you change your mind about an applied treatment on the current segment, click the Reset button that appears for that treatment to remove it and restore the original scores for that segment.
[Normal]: ____________________
[Heading3]: Step 6: Read the segment image, scores, and attributes
[Normal]: The middle column shows the street-level photograph for the current segment. Use Previous and Next to move through segments, or jump to a specific page number.
[Normal]: The right column shows two panels:
[ListBullet]: **Segment Scores** — displays the four CycleRAP risk scores for the current segment: Vehicle-Bicycle (VB), Bicycle-Pedestrian (BP), Single-Bicycle (SB), and Bicycle-Bicycle (BB). When treatments are selected, this panel shows the previewed scores alongside the original scores so you can compare.
[ListBullet]: **Attributes panel** — lists all coded attribute values for the current segment. Toggle **Show Post-Treatment** to switch between the original coded values and the values that would result from the selected treatments.
[Normal]: ____________________
[Heading3]: Step 7: Before and After maps
[Normal]: The two maps at the top of the page give you a project-wide view of how treatments are changing risk across all segments:
[ListBullet]: **Before Treatment map** — always shows the original risk-band colouring for every segment (green = Low, yellow = Medium, orange = High, purple = Extreme). This map does not change as you apply treatments.
[ListBullet]: **After Treatment map** — updates in real time as you apply treatments. Segments that have been treated will shift colour to reflect their new risk band. Segments with no applied treatments remain the same colour as the Before map.
[Normal]: Click any segment on either map to jump directly to that segment's page in the panel below.
[Normal]: ____________________
[Heading3]: Step 8: Generate AI visualisations of proposed improvements
[Normal]: The Treatment Options panel includes two clipboard buttons that help you create visual representations of proposed improvements using external AI image tools:
[ListBullet]: **Copy prompt** — copies a ready-to-use text prompt describing treatments in plain language. This button has a dropdown with two options:
[ListBullet]: **Copy Applied** — copies a prompt based on the treatments already **applied and saved** for this segment.
[ListBullet]: **Copy Selected** — copies a prompt based on the treatments currently **ticked/selected** in the panel (even if not yet applied).
[Normal]: Paste the prompt into an AI image generation tool (such as ChatGPT or DALL·E) to generate a visual mock-up of what the improved path might look like.
[ListBullet]: **Copy image** — copies the current segment photograph directly to your clipboard. Paste it into the same AI tool alongside the prompt so the tool has the actual scene to work from.
[Normal]: Use Copy Selected when you are exploring options and want to preview the AI image before committing. Use Copy Applied when you have already applied treatments and want to generate the final before-and-after visualisation.
[Normal]: ____________________
[Heading3]: Step 9: Review the Overall Treatment Analysis
[Normal]: Scroll to the bottom of the page to see the Overall Treatment Analysis section. It shows two rows of five pie charts each — one row for Before Treatment and one for After Treatment:
[ListBullet]: Each pie chart covers one crash type: **Overall**, **Vehicle-Bicycle (VB)**, **Bicycle-Pedestrian (BP)**, **Single-Bicycle (SB)**, and **Bicycle-Bicycle (BB)**.
[ListBullet]: Each chart shows how the segments in the loaded project are distributed across the four risk bands (Low, Medium, High, Extreme).
[ListBullet]: The After Treatment row updates as you apply treatments, so you can see the project-wide impact of all your decisions at a glance.
[Normal]: Use these charts to get a quick sense of whether the treatments you have applied make a meaningful difference at the project level, not just for individual segments.
[Normal]: ____________________
[Heading3]: Tips and common workflows
[ListBullet]: **Pre and Post tagging** — if you are working with a before-and-after survey pair, tag one project `Pre` and the other `Post`, then load both together. You can then compare the actual post-treatment survey against PSAT's predicted improvement.
[ListBullet]: **Applying a single improvement across a whole project** — switch to **By Treatment** view, find the treatment you want, tick it, then click Apply. The confirmation dialog will list all eligible segments; confirm to apply in bulk.
[ListBullet]: **Undoing all changes** — if you want to start fresh, navigate to each segment that has been treated and click Reset to remove treatments one segment at a time.
[ListBullet]: **Checking what was already applied** — treatments that have already been saved for the current segment are shown with a **green background** in the Treatment Options panel, so you can see at a glance what is already in place.

[Heading2]: 1.1 What PSAT Does
[Normal]: The Path Safety Assessment Tool (PSAT) is an internal web application for assessing cycling and active-mobility corridors using the CycleRAP v2.11 methodology. It combines image-driven coding, GIS lookups, manual review, scoring, and treatment testing in one workflow.
[Normal]: PSAT is used to:
[ListBullet]: turn geotagged survey images into segment-based project data
[ListBullet]: auto-code attributes with CV models, GIS rules, and gradient profiles
[ListBullet]: inspect and manage the GIS layers that power the coding rules
[ListBullet]: review and correct coding in a synchronised image-table-map workspace
[ListBullet]: calculate **BB**, **BP**, **SB**, and **VB** risk scores plus overall risk bands
[ListBullet]: analyse one or more projects together with filters, charts, and exports
[ListBullet]: test treatments and compare before/after risk outcomes, including generating AI image prompts for treatment visualisation

[Heading1]: Architecture Overview
[Normal]: PSAT is a two-container application orchestrated by Docker Compose. The frontend is a React SPA served by nginx, and the backend is a Flask API that owns project storage, GIS lookups, CV inference, scoring, and treatment logic. There is no database; the file system under `data/`, `in/`, and `backend/shapefiles/` is the source of truth.

[Heading2]: 3.2 High-Level Flow
[Normal]: 1. The user creates or selects a project in the frontend.
[Normal]: 2. The frontend calls `/api/projects/*` and `/api/shapefiles/*` as needed.
[Normal]: 3. The backend loads project metadata, geodata, snapshot CSVs, shapefiles, and models lazily.
[Normal]: 4. Coding, analysis, and treatment flows mutate the latest snapshot and persist derived artifacts such as scores, baselines, and autocode metadata.
[Normal]: 5. The frontend rehydrates the updated data through normal REST reads.
[Heading2]: 3.3 Key Ideas
[ListBullet]: projects are stored on disk, not in a database
[ListBullet]: the backend owns project metadata, snapshots, geodata, and image references
[ListBullet]: the frontend reads and writes everything through REST endpoints
[ListBullet]: the Help page reads mirrored markdown from `frontend/public/docs/`
[ListBullet]: polygon-based project creation depends on both source folders in `in/` and GIS helper assets in `backend/shapefiles/`

[Heading3]: 3.42 Lazy Initialization
[Normal]: `get_ctx()` in `projects/routes.py` lazily initializes and caches:
[ListBullet]: `project_manager` over `data/`
[ListBullet]: serializer / attribute-mapping data
[ListBullet]: the legacy CycleRAP interface helpers where still needed
[Normal]: CV models are loaded separately by `_ensure_models_ready()`. Initialization failures are memoized so repeated requests fail fast with HTTP 503 rather than repeatedly blocking on model load.

[Normal]: The metadata model now carries more than name and tags. Relevant fields include:
[ListBullet]: `project_name`
[ListBullet]: `dataset`
[ListBullet]: `source_folders`
[ListBullet]: `tags`
[ListBullet]: `path_key`

[ListBullet]: `date_created`
[ListBullet]: `last_updated`
[Normal]: `dataset` is a single source-folder name for legacy / single-folder projects, or `MULTI_FOLDER_SELECTION` for projects created from multiple folders. `source_folders` is the durable provenance field used by fuzzy search and by newer UI flows.
[Heading3]: 3.62 Snapshot Behavior
[Normal]: Version folders are date-based (`YYYYMMDD`). Multiple saves on the same day update the same dated snapshot rather than creating many sub-daily versions.
[Heading3]: 3.63 Sidecar Directories
[Normal]: Two newer sidecar directories support analysis workflows:
[ListBullet]: `baseline/` stores the attribute baseline used by autocode validation comparisons
[ListBullet]: `autocode/` stores changed-field and field-source metadata for the coding UI
[Heading2]: 3.7 Project Creation Pipeline
[Normal]: Project creation still starts from geotagged source images in `in/`, but it now supports both single-folder and multi-folder/polygon flows.
[Normal]: Backend steps:
[Normal]: 1. enumerate one or more source folders
[Normal]: 2. extract EXIF GPS data from images
[Normal]: 3. optionally filter nodes by a selection polygon
[Normal]: 4. geocode and sample points at the project-creation stage
[Normal]: 5. convert sampled points to LineStrings
[Normal]: 6. copy images into the project image store
[Normal]: 7. create metadata, geodata, and the initial dated snapshot
[Normal]: When multiple source folders are merged, copied image names are namespaced so duplicate filenames do not collide.
[Heading2]: 3.8 Multi-Project Aggregation Pattern
[Normal]: Several frontend pages load multiple projects into one combined view. The usual pattern is:
[ListBullet]: concatenate attributes, results, and geodata into global arrays
[ListBullet]: keep a `projectMap` of project name, start index, and count
[ListBullet]: resolve UI actions back to `{ projectName, localIndex }` when saving, deleting, or treating a row
[Normal]: This pattern is especially important in coding, treatment, and path-analysis views.
[Heading2]: 3.9 Shapefile Management Architecture
[Normal]: The GIS Layers page is backed by the `gis_layers` blueprint. That blueprint:
[ListBullet]: scans `backend/shapefiles/`
[ListBullet]: groups layers by category (subdirectory)
[ListBullet]: can return a selected shapefile as WGS84 GeoJSON
[ListBullet]: validates uploaded ZIPs and replacement files
[ListBullet]: supports upload, preview, replace, and delete operations
[Normal]: This means the same layer inventory drives both the GIS Layers admin page and the backend GIS mapping logic used during coding.
[Heading2]: 3.10 Key Design Choices
[Heading3]: 3.101 API-Only Frontend Communication
[Normal]: The frontend never reads the filesystem directly. Every read/write goes through REST endpoints, which keeps all storage rules and migration logic on the backend.
[Heading3]: 3.102 Native Scoring Path
[Normal]: The current score endpoint uses `calculate_cyclerap_score_native()` instead of Excel COM. Legacy Excel helpers still exist, but native Python scoring is the main path.
[Heading3]: 3.103 CRS Handling
[Normal]: Source image GPS is read in WGS84 (`EPSG:4326`). Stored project geodata is written in Singapore SVY21 (`EPSG:3414`) so geometric distance and width/curvature operations behave sensibly.
[Heading3]: 3.104 Thread Safety
[Normal]: Model loading is protected by a lock and cached error state, which avoids repeated slow failures under concurrent requests.
[Heading2]: 3.11 GIS Layer Definition System
[Normal]: The application maintains a strict schema for GIS layers to ensure compatibility with auto-coding rules.
[ListBullet]: **Location**: `backend/app/services/gis_layer_definition.py`
[ListBullet]: **Structure**: Each layer defines its `layer_name`, `affects_psat_attribute`, and `required_columns`.
[ListBullet]: **Mapping Indices**: Required columns now include 1-based indices (e.g., `LU_DESC (1)`) which are used by both the backend for validation and the frontend for user guidance.

[Heading2]: 5.3 Inference Steps
[Heading3]: 5.31 Step 1 — Path Segmentation
[Normal]: Runs `path_seg.pt` on the full image (confidence ≥ 0.5). If no path (class `0`) is detected, the segment is flagged for manual review.
[Heading3]: 5.32 Step 2 — Light Segregation
[Normal]: Default: `Light Segregation = Present` whenever a path is detected.
[Heading3]: 5.33 Step 3 — Adjacent Road Classification
[Normal]: Runs `adj_road_lane.pt` on the cropped path bounding box (confidence ≥ 0.8):
[Normal]: | Class | Result |
[Normal]: |---|---|
[Normal]: | `1` | Adjacent Road Lane 0–1m = Present, 1–3m = Not Present |
[Normal]: | `2` | Adjacent Road Lane 0–1m = Not Present, 1–3m = Present |
[Normal]: | `0` / low confidence | Uncertain — logged for manual review |
[Heading3]: 5.34 Step 4 — Off-Road Bicycle Path
[Normal]: Runs `off_road_bicycle_path.pt` (confidence ≥ 0.8). If positive:
[ListBullet]: `Facility Type = Off-Road Bicycle Path`
[ListBullet]: `Delineation = Present`
[ListBullet]: Multiple paths detected → `Adjacent Sidewalk 0-1m = Present`
[Heading3]: 5.35 Step 5 — Facility Type Decision
[Normal]: Runs `LTA_FIXEDOBSTACLE_BEST_2.pt` on the full image:
[Normal]: | Condition | Facility Type |
[Normal]: |---|---|
[Normal]: | Bus stop detected (labels 1, 4) | Sidewalk |
[Normal]: | Bottom half >90% pathway AND pathway > road | Multi-Use Path |
[Normal]: | Road confined to one side (>80%) | Sidewalk |
[Normal]: | Only pathway, no road | Sidewalk |
[Normal]: | Default | Mixed Traffic Road Lane |
[Heading3]: 5.36 Step 6 — Fixed Obstacle & Delineation
[Normal]: Second pass checks whether obstacle masks overlap the path mask:
[Normal]: | Label Set | Attribute Set |
[Normal]: |---|---|
[Normal]: | `{1, 2, 4, 6, 7}` intersect path | Fixed Obstacle on Facility = Present |
[Normal]: | Label `9` intersects path | Non-Fixed Obstacle on Facility = Present |
[Normal]: | `{0, 1, 2, 5}` on path | Delineation = Present |
[Normal]: ____________________

[Heading2]: 6.1 Overview
[Normal]: CycleRAP produces four independent risk scores for each road segment, each representing a distinct crash scenario:
[Normal]: | Score | Crash type | Description |
[Normal]: |---|---|---|
[Normal]: | BB | Bicyclist–Bicyclist | Conflict between cyclists (and with light vehicles) |
[Normal]: | BP | Bicyclist–Pedestrian | Conflict between a cyclist and a pedestrian |
[Normal]: | SB | Single Bicyclist | Cyclist departing facility and striking a fixed/severe hazard |
[Normal]: | VB | Vehicle–Bicyclist | Conflict between a motor vehicle and a cyclist |
[Normal]: Each score is a continuous real number. Scores are then classified into one of four risk bands.
[Normal]: An Overall Risk Level (the sum of all four scores) and an Overall Risk Level Band (the maximum of the four component bands) are also computed.
[Normal]: ____________________
[Heading2]: 6.2 Risk Bands
[Heading3]: 6.21 BB, BP, SB
[Normal]: | Band | Label | Score range |
[Normal]: |---|---|---|
[Normal]: | 1 | Low | < 5 |
[Normal]: | 2 | Medium | 5 – 10 |
[Normal]: | 3 | High | 10 – 20 |
[Normal]: | 4 | Extreme | > 20 |
[Heading3]: 6.22 VB
[Normal]: | Band | Label | Score range |
[Normal]: |---|---|---|
[Normal]: | 1 | Low | < 10 |
[Normal]: | 2 | Medium | 10 – 25 |
[Normal]: | 3 | High | 25 – 60 |
[Normal]: | 4 | Extreme | > 60 |
[Heading3]: 6.23 Overall Risk Level Band
[Normal]: Equal to the maximum band across BB, BP, SB, and VB for that segment. This means a single extreme sub-score elevates the overall band to Extreme regardless of the others.
[Normal]: ____________________
[Heading2]: 6.3 Algorithm: Component Formulas
[Normal]: The algorithm builds four intermediate components (`CM3`, `CM16`, `CM25`, `CM40`) and then combines them into the final scores.
[Heading3]: 6.31 CM3 — Main Cycling Environment Risk
[Normal]: ```
[Normal]: CM3 = (product of CU factors) ^ (1 + CQ_sum × 0.1)
[Normal]: ```
[Normal]: *Layman's explanation: This math formula is used to calculate the risk score for the main cycling environment based on various factors like surface and lighting.*
[Normal]: CU factors (continuous risk multipliers):
[Normal]: | Factor | Attribute | Effect |
[Normal]: |---|---|---|
[Normal]: | Loose/slippery surface | `Loose or slippery surface` | 1.5 if Present |
[Normal]: | Delineation | `Delineation` | 1.2 if Not Present |
[Normal]: | Facility width | `Facility Width per Direction` | 1.8/1.5/1.0 for Very Narrow/Narrow/Wide |
[Normal]: | Flow direction | `Flow Direction` | 1.5 if Two Way |
[Normal]: | Width restriction | `Width Restriction` | 1.2 if Present |
[Normal]: | Grade | `Grade` | 1.2 if ≥5 Degrees |
[Normal]: | Cargo bikes | `Observed proportion of cargo bikes` | 1.2 if Moderate to high |
[Normal]: | Pedestrian flow | `Peak pedestrian flow` | 1.2 if Low; 1.5 if Moderate to high |
[Normal]: | Bicycle flow | `Peak bicycle/LV traffic flow` | 1.2 if Moderate to high |
[Normal]: | Speed differential | `Bicycle/LV speed differential` | 1.2 if ≥10 km/h |
[Normal]: | Curvature | `Curvature` | 1.5 if Sharp Turn Present |
[Normal]: | Street lighting | `Street Lighting` | 1.2 if Not Present |
[Normal]: CQ triggers (condition flags that increase the exponent):
[Normal]: | Trigger | Attribute | Fires when |
[Normal]: |---|---|---|
[Normal]: | Surface deformation | `Major Surface Deformation or Drain Opening` | Present |
[Normal]: | Fixed obstacle | `Fixed Obstacle on Facility` | Present |
[Normal]: | Non-fixed obstacle | `Non-Fixed Obstacle on Facility` | Present |
[Normal]: | Adjacent parking 0–1m | `Adjacent Vehicle Parking 0-1m` | Present |
[Normal]: | Intersection crossing | `Intersection or Road Crossing` | Present |
[Normal]: | Property access | `Property Access` | Present |
[Normal]: | Intersecting facility | `Intersecting Bicycle Facility` | Present |
[Normal]: | Pedestrian crossing | `Pedestrian Crossing` | Present |
[Heading3]: 6.32 CM16 — Departure and Fall Scenarios
[Normal]: Only fires if at least one of: Loose/slippery surface, Grade ≥5°, or Curvature = Sharp Turn.
[Heading3]: 6.33 CM25 — Speed-Related Incidents
[Normal]: Only fires if at least one of: Tram/train rails present, Surface deformation present.
[Heading3]: 6.34 CM40 — Vehicle Interaction
[Normal]: Only fires if at least one of: Intersection crossing, Property access, Adjacent road 0–1m, Adjacent road 1–3m, Facility type = Mixed Traffic, Intersection approach = Shared.
[Heading3]: 6.35 Final Score Combination
[Normal]: The four components combine into final scores as follows:
[Normal]: ```
[Normal]: cj3  = CM3 + CM16   # combined conflict exposure
[Normal]: cj25 = CM25         # fall hazard modifier
[Normal]: cj40 = CM40         # shared space conflict modifier
[Normal]: BB = cj3 × (bicycle_speed × grade × speed_differential × cargo_bike factors)
[Normal]: BP = BB_component × sidewalk_condition
[Normal]: + BB_component × pedestrian_condition
[Normal]: (each term is 0 if the condition is absent)
[Normal]: SB = cj3 × severity_factors                  [departure gate — 0 if no departure condition]
[Normal]: + (cj3 + cj25) × fall_severity_factors    [fall gate — 0 if no fall condition]
[Normal]: VB = (cj3 + cj25) × factors   [fall/conflict — 0 if condition absent]
[Normal]: + cj3 × factors             [departure — 0 if condition absent]
[Normal]: + cj40 ÷ 3                  [intersection term 1 — 0 if condition absent]
[Normal]: + cj40 ÷ 3                  [intersection term 2 — 0 if condition absent]
[Normal]: + cj40 ÷ 3                  [intersection term 3 — 0 if condition absent]
[Normal]: ```
[Normal]: *Layman's explanation: These equations combine the different risk factors calculated above into the final safety scores you see on the screen.*
[Normal]: Key takeaway: BB and BP are driven entirely by CM3 + CM16. SB additionally incorporates CM25 for fall scenarios. VB is the only score that uses CM40 (shared-space and intersection conflicts).
[Normal]: ____________________
[Heading2]: 6.4 Worked Example: CM3
[Normal]: The following example traces a single segment through the CM3 calculation by hand.
[Normal]: Segment attributes:
[Normal]: | Attribute | Value | Code |
[Normal]: |---|---|---|
[Normal]: | Loose or slippery surface | Not Present | 2 |
[Normal]: | Delineation | Not Present | 2 |
[Normal]: | Facility Width per Direction | Narrow | 2 |
[Normal]: | Flow Direction | One Way | 1 |
[Normal]: | Width Restriction | Not Present | 2 |
[Normal]: | Grade | < 5 Degrees | 1 |
[Normal]: | Observed proportion of cargo bikes | Low | 1 |
[Normal]: | Peak pedestrian flow | None | 1 |
[Normal]: | Peak bicycle/LV traffic flow | Low | 1 |
[Normal]: | Bicycle/LV speed differential | < 10 km/h | 1 |
[Normal]: | Curvature | Sharp Turn Present | 1 |
[Normal]: | Street Lighting | Present | 1 |
[Normal]: | All CQ-trigger fields | Not Present / N/A | 2 |
[Normal]: Step 1 — Build the CU product:
[Normal]: ```
[Normal]: CU = 1.0  (loose surface: Not Present → 1.0)
[Normal]: × 1.2  (delineation:  Not Present → 1.2)
[Normal]: × 1.5  (width:        Narrow      → 1.5)
[Normal]: × 1.0  (flow:         One Way     → 1.0)
[Normal]: × 1.0  (width restr:  Not Present → 1.0)
[Normal]: × 1.0  (grade:        <5°         → 1.0)
[Normal]: × 1.0  (cargo bikes:  Low         → 1.0)
[Normal]: × 1.0  (ped flow:     None        → 1.0)
[Normal]: × 1.0  (bike flow:    Low         → 1.0)
[Normal]: × 1.0  (speed diff:   <10 km/h    → 1.0)
[Normal]: × 1.5  (curvature:    Sharp Turn  → 1.5)
[Normal]: × 1.0  (lighting:     Present     → 1.0)
[Normal]: = 1.8
[Normal]: ```
[Normal]: Step 2 — Count the CQ triggers:
[Normal]: All eight CQ-trigger fields (surface deformation, fixed obstacle, non-fixed obstacle, adjacent parking 0–1m, intersection crossing, property access, intersecting facility, pedestrian crossing) are coded as Not Present. CQ_sum = 0.
[Normal]: Step 3 — Apply the formula:
[Normal]: ```
[Normal]: CM3 = CU_product ^ (1 + CQ_sum × 0.1)
[Normal]: = 1.8 ^ (1 + 0 × 0.1)
[Normal]: = 1.8 ^ 1.0
[Normal]: = 1.8
[Normal]: ```
[Normal]: *Layman's explanation: This example shows how the computer calculates a risk score of 1.8 for a specific road segment.*
[Normal]: Now add one CQ trigger — set Intersection or Road Crossing = Present (1):
[ListBullet]: CQ_sum = 1 (intersection_crossing fires)
[Normal]: ```
[Normal]: CM3 = 1.8 ^ (1 + 1 × 0.1)
[Normal]: = 1.8 ^ 1.1
[Normal]: ≈ 1.91
[Normal]: ```
[Normal]: The exponent increases by 0.1 per active CQ trigger, compounding the base risk. Eight simultaneous triggers would raise the exponent to 1.8, producing `1.8^1.8 ≈ 2.66`.
[Normal]: Road traffic volume is converted to a risk factor using a stepped lookup table:
[Normal]: | AADT threshold | Risk factor |
[Normal]: |---|---|
[Normal]: | < 100 | 0.25 |
[Normal]: | 100 – 499 | 0.25 |
[Normal]: | 500 – 1499 | 0.50 |
[Normal]: | 1500 – 2499 | 0.75 |
[Normal]: | 2500 – 4999 | 1.00 |
[Normal]: | 5000 – 7499 | 1.07 |
[Normal]: | 7500 – 9999 | 1.14 |
[Normal]: | 10000 – 12499 | 1.23 |
[Normal]: | 12500 – 14999 | 1.31 |
[Normal]: | 15000 – 17499 | 1.40 |
[Normal]: | 17500 – 19999 | 1.50 |
[Normal]: | 20000 – 22499 | 1.61 |
[Normal]: | 22500 – 24999 | 1.72 |
[Normal]: | 25000 – 29999 | 1.84 |
[Normal]: | 30000 – 34999 | 1.97 |
[Normal]: | 35000 – 39999 | 2.10 |
[Normal]: | ≥ 40000 | 2.25 |
[Heading3]: 6.41 Road Speed Risk Factor
[Normal]: Operating speed is converted via a sigmoid formula:
[Normal]: ```
[Normal]: lookup_speed = round(speed) - 1
[Normal]: risk = 1 + 27.82 / (1 + exp(5.84 − 0.091 × lookup_speed))
[Normal]: ```
[Normal]: *Layman's explanation: This formula is used to calculate how the speed of vehicles on the road affects the safety score.*
[Normal]: > Speed is first rounded to the nearest integer, then decremented by 1 before the sigmoid is applied. Returns 1.0 if speed ≤ 1.

[Heading2]: 6.8 Updating the CycleRAP Algorithm
[Normal]: When CycleRAP releases an updated risk scoring model (e.g., transitioning from v2.11 to a newer version), the development team must update the scoring module.
[Normal]: Administrators are advised to contact developers when a new model is released. The update process generally involves the following steps:
[Normal]: 1. Reviewing the New Specifications: Obtain the new CycleRAP methodology documentation or Excel reference tool.
[Normal]: 2. Updating `LOOKUP_TABLES`: Modify the attribute-to-risk-factor dictionaries in `backend/app/services/cyclerap_scoring.py` to match the new multipliers.
[Normal]: 3. Adjusting Formula Equations: If new formulas are introduced for intermediate components (e.g., `CM3`, `CM16`, `CM25`, `CM40`) or final scores (`BB`, `BP`, `SB`, `VB`), update the corresponding `calculate_cmX()` and `calculate_cyclerap_score()` functions.
[Normal]: 4. Modifying Attribute Definitions: If the new model introduces new fields or changes the allowed values (enums) for existing fields, update the data model, including frontend forms and backend shapefile ingestion validation.
[Normal]: 5. Testing: Validate the updated Python output against the new official CycleRAP reference tool (typically using a test batch of road segments) to ensure full fidelity.
[Heading1]: Treatment Configuration
[Normal]: Treatments in PSAT are used to simulate safety improvements on road segments. Each treatment has a set of triggers (conditions that must be met for the treatment to be applicable) and effects (changes to the road attributes if the treatment is applied).
[Normal]: ____________________
[Heading2]: 6.9 Structure of a Treatment
[Normal]: Treatments are defined as a list of dictionaries in the backend. Each dictionary follows this schema:
[Normal]: ```python
[Normal]: {
[Normal]: "id": 1,
[Normal]: "name": "Upgrade to on-road bicycle lane with light segregation",
[Normal]: "triggers": [
[Normal]: {"Facility Type": [5], "Light Segregation": [2]},
[Normal]: {"Facility Type": [6], "Light Segregation": [2]}
[Normal]: ],
[Normal]: "effects": {"Facility Type": 4, "Light Segregation": 1, "Facility access": 1}
[Normal]: }
[Normal]: ```
[Heading3]: 6.91 Fields Explained
[Normal]: | Field | Type | Description |
[Normal]: |---|---|---|
[Normal]: | `id` | Integer | A unique 1-based identifier for the treatment. |
[Normal]: | `name` | String | The display name shown in the Treatment Page. |
[Normal]: | `triggers` | List[Dict] | A list of condition sets. If any dictionary in the list matches the segment's attributes, the treatment is applicable (OR logic between list items). |
[Normal]: | `effects` | Dict | A dictionary of field-value pairs. When applied, these attributes are updated to the specified values (1-based indices from the attribute dropdowns). |
[Heading3]: 6.92 Trigger Logic (AND/OR)
[ListBullet]: **OR Logic**: The `triggers` list is a collection of alternative conditions. If a segment satisfies any one of these dictionaries, the treatment becomes a "Recommended Treatment".
[ListBullet]: **AND Logic**: Inside a single trigger dictionary, all specified fields must match the segment's current values. For example, `{"Facility Type": [5], "Light Segregation": [2]}` means the segment must be a Road Shoulder (5) **AND** have no Light Segregation (2).
[Normal]: ____________________
[Heading2]: 6.10 How to Add a New Treatment
[Normal]: To add a new treatment to the system, follow these steps:
[Normal]: 1.  Locate the Definitions: Open `backend/app/api/projects/routes.py`.
[Normal]: 2.  Find the `TREATMENTS` List: Search for the `TREATMENTS = [...]` variable near the top of the file.
[Normal]: 3.  Append a New Entry: Add a new dictionary to the end of the list.
[ListBullet]: Ensure the `id` is the next available integer.
[ListBullet]: Use the correct attribute names as keys (matching the CSV headers).
[ListBullet]: Use the numeric indices for values (e.g., `1` for Present, `2` for Not Present).
[Normal]: 4.  Restart the Backend: If running in Docker, rebuild or restart the container to pick up the changes.

[Heading1]: Common Issues
[Normal]: This page focuses on the issues that show up most often in current PSAT workflows, especially around Docker setup, polygon-based project creation, and GIS-layer management.
[Heading2]: 8.1 Docker and startup
[Heading3]: 8.11 Docker Desktop is not running
[Normal]: Symptom: `docker compose up --build` fails immediately with a Docker pipe / connection error.
[Normal]: Fix: Start Docker Desktop and wait for it to report that the engine is running, then retry.
[Heading3]: 8.12 Port 80 or 8000 is already in use
[Normal]: Symptom: The stack starts partially, but the frontend or backend is unreachable.
[Normal]: Fix: Stop the conflicting service or change the port mappings in `docker-compose.yml`.
[Heading3]: 8.13 Backend health check fails after build
[Normal]: Symptom: `http://localhost:8000/api/ping` does not respond.
[Normal]: Fix: Check `docker compose logs backend` first. The most common causes are:
[ListBullet]: missing `backend/models/`
[ListBullet]: missing `backend/shapefiles/`
[ListBullet]: a bad local edit that breaks imports
[Heading2]: 8.2 Models and shapefiles
[Heading3]: 8.21 Auto-code fails because models are missing
[Normal]: Symptom: CV auto-code returns 503 or logs show `missing path_seg.pt`.
[Normal]: Fix: Copy the shared model files into `backend/models/` and rebuild.
[Heading3]: 8.22 GIS auto-code or GIS Layers page is empty
[Normal]: Symptom: GIS-assisted coding returns nothing, or the GIS Layers page has no usable layers.
[Normal]: Fix: Confirm the shapefile tree exists under `backend/shapefiles/` and contains the expected layer files, not just empty folders.
[Heading2]: 8.3 `in/` folder and source images
[Heading3]: 8.31 `in/` was created by Docker instead of manually
[Normal]: Symptom: File writes into `in/` fail or behave strangely.
[Normal]: Fix: Stop Docker, recreate `in/` manually from the host, then restart.
[Heading3]: 8.32 Images are directly under `in/` instead of inside subfolders
[Normal]: Symptom: The folder dropdown is empty or project creation fails.
[Normal]: Fix: Put images inside subdirectories of `in/`, one folder per source road / survey batch.
[Heading3]: 8.33 Images have no GPS EXIF data
[Normal]: Symptom: Project creation fails, or you get zero usable segments.
[Normal]: Fix: The source images must contain GPS EXIF metadata. Screenshots, stripped images, and many exported image sets will not work.
[Heading2]: 8.4 Polygon and road selection
[Heading3]: 8.41 Polygon selection returns planning areas instead of roads
[Normal]: Symptom: The map selection tool only returns high-level planning-area names, or the road list looks incomplete.
[Normal]: Cause: The backend falls back when it cannot get better road matches from `road_reference.csv` and road-name shapefiles.
[Normal]: Fix:
[Normal]: 1. Make sure `backend/shapefiles/road_reference.csv` exists.
[Normal]: 2. Regenerate it after adding or renaming folders in `in/`:
[Normal]: ```bash
[Normal]: cd backend
[Normal]: python generate_road_reference.py
[Normal]: ```
[Normal]: 3. Confirm the road-name shapefiles are present under `backend/shapefiles/`.
[Heading3]: 8.42 Selected roads are marked unavailable
[Normal]: Symptom: The Create Project page shows roads, but some are flagged as missing and block creation.
[Normal]: Fix: Those roads do not currently have matching local folders under `in/`. Either:
[ListBullet]: add the missing source folders and images
[ListBullet]: or deselect the unavailable roads before creating the project
[Heading3]: 8.43 Create Project says no geotagged images were found inside the polygon
[Normal]: Symptom: `POST /api/projects/folders` returns a 400 error about no geotagged images inside the selected polygon.
[Normal]: Cause: The selected roads may exist, but none of their sampled GPS points survived the polygon filter.
[Normal]: Fix:
[ListBullet]: redraw a larger polygon
[ListBullet]: verify the images in those folders have GPS EXIF data
[ListBullet]: regenerate `road_reference.csv` if the source folders changed recently
[Heading2]: 8.5 Project metadata and search
[Heading3]: 8.51 Searching by road name does not return the expected project
[Normal]: Symptom: The Projects, Treatment, or Path Analysis page does not surface a project when you search for a road.
[Normal]: Cause: Newer projects store `source_folders` explicitly. Older projects are reconstructed from image namespaces on a best-effort basis, which may be incomplete if images predate the multi-folder naming convention.
[Normal]: Fix: For older projects, check the actual project metadata or recreate the project if road-level provenance is critical.
[Heading3]: 8.52 Project name cannot contain underscores
[Normal]: Symptom: Project creation returns `Project name cannot contain underscores (_)`.
[Normal]: Fix: Use spaces, hyphens, or camel case instead.
[Heading2]: 8.6 Scoring and coding
[Heading3]: 8.61 Scores do not update after save
[Normal]: Symptom: The attributes save succeeds but the displayed scores look stale.
[Normal]: Fix: Check backend logs for scoring errors. `PUT /api/projects/<name>/attributes` recalculates and persists scores automatically.
[Heading3]: 8.62 First auto-code request is very slow
[Normal]: Expected behavior: The first CV request loads the YOLO models into memory. Subsequent requests are much faster.
[Heading2]: 8.7 Documentation drift
[Heading3]: 8.71 The repository docs were updated, but the Help page still shows old content
[Normal]: Cause: The Help page reads mirrored markdown from `frontend/public/docs/`, not directly from `docs/`.
[Normal]: Fix: Sync both locations whenever docs change.
[Heading2]: 8.8 Data recovery
[Heading3]: 8.81 Projects disappear after container restart
[Normal]: Symptom: `docker compose down` followed by `up` appears to lose projects.
[Normal]: Fix: Confirm that `./data:/app/data` is still present in `docker-compose.yml` and that the host `data/` directory still exists.
[Heading3]: 8.82 A project was deleted accidentally
[Normal]: Cause: Project deletion removes the project directory recursively.
[Normal]: Fix: Restore it from a filesystem backup if available. There is no soft-delete layer.

[Title]: PSAT Administrator Guide
[Normal]: This section provides instructions for system administrators on how to deploy, manage, and update the Path Safety Assessment Tool (PSAT).
[ListNumber]: Deployment & Infrastructure
[ListBullet]: Starting the App: The application is typically orchestrated via Docker Compose. Run 'docker compose up --build' to start both the Flask backend and the React frontend. For direct local launching, you can use the Run-PSAT.bat script.
[ListBullet]: Data Persistence: User-created projects, images, and results are stored in the data/ directory, which is bind-mounted to the backend. Backing up this folder will backup all user work across the system.
[ListNumber]: Managing Machine Learning Models
[ListBullet]: YOLO Weights: The computer-vision prediction models are stored in backend/models/. To deploy a newly trained model, replace the existing .pt files and restart the backend container.
[ListBullet]: Hardware Configuration: The backend loads PyTorch models into memory on initialization. Ensure the host machine has adequate RAM. For GPU acceleration, CUDA drivers must be properly configured.
[ListNumber]: Managing GIS Data Layers
[ListBullet]: Storage Location: The CycleRAP contextual GIS infrastructure shapefiles are stored under backend/shapefiles/.
[ListBullet]: Updating via UI: Administrators can now use the 'Update GIS Layer' button in the sidebar to add or replace layers. This UI handles file validation and ensures that all mandatory companion files (.shx, .dbf, etc.) are present.
[ListBullet]: Replacement Safety: The 'Replace GIS Layer' workflow includes a search filter for quick navigation and a compatibility check that verifies the new file's column structure against the existing layer definition.
[ListBullet]: Column Mapping: Ensure that any new GIS data follows the required column indices documented in the 'Gis Layers' dashboard (e.g., column index 1 for LU_DESC).
[ListNumber]: Troubleshooting & Health
[ListBullet]: Logs: If auto-coding fails, check the server output via 'docker compose logs -f backend' to view full Python stack traces.
[ListBullet]: Health Endpoints: Query /api/health or /api/ping to verify the backend is responsive and CV models loaded correctly.
[ListNumber]: User Accounts & Sign-In
[ListBullet]: PSAT uses a local profile system. Each user creates a named profile secured by a 4–12 digit PIN. Profiles and their associated projects persist on disk — users can always sign back in on the same device to access their previously saved projects.
[ListBullet]: How a user signs in
[ListBullet]: 1. Open PSAT. The Landing Page shows all profiles registered on this device.
[ListBullet]: 2. Select your profile from the list on the right-hand panel.
[ListBullet]: 3. Click "Start As <Name>". A PIN prompt appears if this is not the currently active session.
[ListBullet]: 4. Enter your PIN and click the confirmation button. You are now logged in.
[ListBullet]: 5. Your projects appear on the Projects page (Home). All previously saved work is available immediately.
[ListBullet]: Creating a new account
[ListBullet]: 1. On the Landing Page, click "Create Profile".
[ListBullet]: 2. Enter a profile name, your division, and a 4–12 digit numeric PIN.
[ListBullet]: 3. Click "Create Profile". The profile is saved and you are automatically logged in.
[ListBullet]: Switching accounts
[ListBullet]: Click "Log Out" in the sidebar at any time. You are returned to the Landing Page, where you can select a different profile.
[ListBullet]: Note: The active session is device-local. Each browser tab / app window shares the same active profile on that device. Logging out from one window affects all open tabs.
[ListNumber]: Admin Dashboard — Usage Tracking
[ListBullet]: The Admin Dashboard lets you monitor usage without needing to be physically present. It shows total accounts created, logins today, all-time login totals, a daily login bar chart, and a per-account breakdown.
[ListBullet]: Accessing the Admin Dashboard
[ListBullet]: 1. Log in to any profile on the device.
[ListBullet]: 2. Navigate to the Projects page (Home).
[ListBullet]: 3. Click "Admin Dashboard" in the lower section of the left-hand sidebar.
[ListBullet]: 4. The dashboard loads immediately with live data from the local database.
[ListBullet]: Note: The Admin Dashboard is accessible to any user who can reach the PSAT URL. It does not require a separate admin PIN. Restrict network access to control who can view it.
[ListBullet]: Reading the summary cards
[ListBullet]: Three cards appear at the top of the dashboard:
[ListBullet]: 1. Total Accounts Created — the number of profiles registered on this installation.
[ListBullet]: 2. Logins Today — successful sign-in events recorded since midnight UTC on this device.
[ListBullet]: 3. Total Logins (All Time) — the cumulative count of all login events ever recorded.
[ListBullet]: Daily login chart
[ListBullet]: The bar chart shows login frequency over a rolling window. Use the day-range tabs (7d / 14d / 30d / 90d) above the chart to change the period. Each bar represents one calendar day; hover to see the exact count.
[ListBullet]: All Accounts table
[ListBullet]: Below the chart, a table lists every registered profile with the following columns:
[ListBullet]: 1. Name — the display name of the profile.
[ListBullet]: 2. Division — the team or department entered at account creation.
[ListBullet]: 3. Projects — the number of projects currently saved under this profile.
[ListBullet]: 4. Logins — the total number of times this account has logged in (all time).
[ListBullet]: 5. Created — the date and time the profile was created.
[ListBullet]: 6. Last Active — the date and time of the most recent login or action.
[ListBullet]: Refreshing the data
[ListBullet]: Click the "⟳ Refresh" button at the top right of the dashboard to reload the latest data from the database without leaving the page.
[ListBullet]: Remote access (without being physically present)
[ListBullet]: If PSAT is deployed on a shared server or behind a VPN, the Admin Dashboard is accessible from any machine that can reach the PSAT web address. Navigate to:
[ListBullet]: http://<server-address>:<port>/admin
[ListBullet]: No additional login is required — the page loads immediately. For security, ensure that network-level access controls (firewall, VPN) restrict who can reach the PSAT server.
[ListBullet]: Note: All login events are stored in profiles/telemetry.sqlite3 on the server. This file can also be queried directly with any SQLite client (e.g. DB Browser for SQLite) for custom reports. The relevant table is activity_events; filter on event_type = "profile_login".
